07 //Legumes & Nitrogen Fixation

Key Concept
Fabaceae family + Rhizobium symbiosis → N₂ fixation
Fabaceae
Root Nodules
Nitrogenase
Leghaemoglobin

Nitrogen Fixation — Step by Step

1
Rhizobium in soil detects flavonoids released by legume roots
2
Bacteria attach to root hair → enter root cells
3
Root forms root nodules (swellings) to house bacteria
4
Rhizobium converts atmospheric N₂ → NH₃ (ammonia) using enzyme nitrogenase
5
Plant gets fixed nitrogen for amino acids, proteins, chlorophyll
6
Bacteria get carbohydrates from plant in return
⚠️
Critical Biochemistry
Nitrogenase requires anaerobic (O₂-free) conditions → root nodules have leghaemoglobin (pink pigment) that binds excess O₂ to protect nitrogenase.
When soil N is already high → FUN factor shuts down nodule formation.

Aquatic/waterlogged plants. Aerenchyma for buoyancy. Stomata on upper surface only.

Free-floating: Water Hyacinth, Pistia, Lemna
Rooted floating: Lotus, Water Lily
Submerged: Hydrilla, Vallisneria
Aerenchyma
🌵
Xerophytes
Dry/desert plants. Deep roots, waxy cuticle, succulent tissues, reduced leaves → spines, CAM photosynthesis (stomata open at night).

Cacti, Aloe, Agave, Acacia, Prosopis (Khejri), Calotropis
CAM Photosynthesis
🌿
Mesophytes
Moderate moisture — most common plant type. Normal stomata on lower leaf surface.

High-salinity environments. Pneumatophores for O₂. Viviparous germination in mangroves.
