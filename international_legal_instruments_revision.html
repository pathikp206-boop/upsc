UPSC revision note on international legal instruments — terms, meanings, formality, and exam shortcuts

International legal instruments — revision note UPSC / IR / International law
Term & formality
Meaning, scope & key point
Treaty
Highest formality
Umbrella term for any binding written international agreement governed by international law.
In specific use, reserved for matters of greater importance or solemnity. Requires ratification. All the terms below are technically sub-types of treaty.
Convention
High formality
Formal multilateral treaty, typically with many parties, often negotiated under an international organisation.
In a generic sense can mean any treaty. Sets broad norms or standards.
E.g. Vienna Convention on Diplomatic Relations (1961), UNCLOS (1982)
Charter
High formality
Particularly formal and solemn instrument — usually the founding treaty of an international organisation.
Carries constitutional weight within the organisation it creates.
E.g. UN Charter (1945), Charter of the Organisation of American States
Protocol
Medium formality
Usually connected to an existing treaty — adds, implements, supplements, or amends it. Can also stand alone.
Five uses: interpret a treaty · create optional extra obligations · implement a framework convention · amend an earlier treaty · supplement it.
E.g. Montreal Protocol (operationalises Vienna Convention on Ozone Layer), Kyoto Protocol
Agreement
Medium formality
Generic term for any international agreement; specifically, often a narrower, technical, or bilateral instrument.
Less solemn than a treaty or convention. May or may not require ratification depending on its content.
E.g. TRIPS Agreement, Free Trade Agreements
Declaration
Variable — may be non-binding
Statement of principles or aspirations. May be non-binding, but can be binding if parties clearly intended it.
The title alone does not decide legal effect — intent of parties is what matters.
E.g. Universal Declaration of Human Rights (non-binding), Declaration of Paris 1856 (binding)
Memorandum of Understanding (MOU)
Least formal
Less formal instrument for operational or technical arrangements. Usually does not require ratification.
Used for working-level cooperation between governments or agencies. Not normally published in treaty series.
E.g. India–US defence cooperation MOUs, bilateral space-agency MOUs
Exam shortcut — one-line recall
TreatyWidest umbrella term
ConventionLarge multilateral treaty
CharterFounding document of an organisation
ProtocolAdd-on or amendment to existing treaty
AgreementNarrower, often bilateral instrument
DeclarationPrinciples / aspirations; may be non-binding
MOUPractical working arrangement; no ratification
Key examiner point: There is no perfectly rigid nomenclature in international law. The title of an instrument alone does not determine its legal status or binding nature — what matters is the intention of the parties, its content, and whether it is registered under Article 102 of the UN Charter. Context always overrides the label.
